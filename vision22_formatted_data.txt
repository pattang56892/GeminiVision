    arithmetic progression) `a1, a2, ..., a(4m+2)`, where 'm' is a positive
    integer and the common difference is non-zero. This is the base sequence
    we're working with.
    * **Removal and Divisibility:**  A sequence is considered "(i, j)-divisible"
    if, after removing the terms `ai` and `aj` (where i < j), the remaining `4m`
    terms can be split into *m* groups of 4, such that each of these groups of 4
    forms an arithmetic sequence.
    * **The Problem:** Sub-question (3) asks:
        * We randomly choose two indices, `i` and `j`, from the set {1, 2, ...,
    4m+2}, with the condition that `i < j`.
        * We want to calculate `P_m`, the probability that the sequence `a1, a2,
    ..., a(4m+2)` is (i,j)-divisible.
        * We need to prove that `P_m > 1/8`.
    **Logic and Analysis**
    1. **Counting the Possibilities:**
       * First, we need to figure out the total number of ways to choose two
    distinct indices `i` and `j` from 1 to 4m+2 where `i<j`. This is a
    combination problem. We have (4m+2) numbers to choose from, and we need to
    choose two of them. The total number of possibilities is given by: (4m+2
    choose 2) = (4m+2) * (4m+1) / 2.
    2. **Conditions for Divisibility:**
       * The core challenge lies in determining which specific pairs (i,j) will
    result in an (i,j)-divisible sequence.
       * For a sequence to be (i,j)-divisible, the remaining terms (4m terms)
    after removing ai and aj must be capable of being grouped into 'm'
    arithmetic sequences of length 4.  This implies that the indices *must* be
    removed in a way that preserves the underlying arithmetic sequence structure
    in the leftover terms.
       * Given the original sequence is an arithmetic sequence, the critical
    point is that if we remove two terms from an arithmetic sequence, the *only
    way* the remaining terms can still be grouped into arithmetic sequences of 4
    is if the indices that are removed, when considered modulo 4, are not equal.
    In other words, the indices *i* and *j* must have different remainders when
    divided by 4.
       * This condition must be met because consecutive terms in an arithmetic
    sequence always have the difference of '1' at the index, and we must have
    sequences of length '4'.  If 'i' and 'j' leave the same remainder upon
    division by 4, the remaining terms cannot be grouped to from '4' item
    sequences with equal index gaps (hence an arithmetic sequence).
    3. **Counting Favorable Outcomes**
       * Let us divide our set of indices {1,2,..., 4m+2} into categories based
    on their remainder when divided by 4. These are the remainders 0, 1, 2 and
    3.
       * The number of terms for each remainder would be `m`, and remainder '1'
    and '2' would have an additional term each. That is,
         * Remainder '1': `m+1` terms (1, 5, 9 ... 4m+1)
         * Remainder '2': `m+1` terms (2, 6, 10 ... 4m+2)
         * Remainder '3': `m` terms (3, 7, 11 ... 4m-1)
         * Remainder '0': `m` terms (4, 8, 12, ... 4m)
       * Now to count combinations of indices, i and j, such that they have
    different remainders we sum the cases where we pick two indices, one from
    each pair of different remainders.
         * (Remainder 1 and Remainder 2): (m+1)*(m+1) = m^2 +2m + 1
         * (Remainder 1 and Remainder 3): (m+1)*m = m^2 +m
         * (Remainder 1 and Remainder 0): (m+1)*m = m^2 +m
         * (Remainder 2 and Remainder 3): (m+1)*m = m^2 +m
         * (Remainder 2 and Remainder 0): (m+1)*m = m^2 +m
         * (Remainder 3 and Remainder 0): m*m = m^2
       * Sum these cases up to calculate the total number of (i,j) pairs where
    *i* and *j* do not have the same remainder when divided by 4:
         Favorable outcomes = (m^2 +2m + 1) + 4(m^2 + m) + m^2 =  6m^2 + 6m + 1
    4. **Calculating the Probability:**
       *   The probability `P_m` is the ratio of favorable outcomes to total
    outcomes:
         *    `P_m` =  (6m^2 + 6m + 1) / ((4m+2)*(4m+1)/2)
         *    `P_m` =  (2*(6m^2 + 6m + 1)) / ((4m+2)*(4m+1))
         *    `P_m` =  (12m^2 + 12m + 2) / (16m^2 + 12m + 2)
    5. **Proving Pm > 1/8**
     *  We need to show that (12m^2 + 12m + 2) / (16m^2 + 12m + 2) > 1/8
     *  Multiplying both sides by (8*(16m^2 + 12m + 2))  gives:
     *  8(12m^2 + 12m + 2) > 16m^2 + 12m + 2
     *  96m^2 + 96m + 16 > 16m^2 + 12m + 2
     *   80m^2 + 84m + 14 > 0
     *   For m>=1, all terms are positive, so this condition holds.
     *  This also shows that our inequality holds even for m=1.  (80+84+14 > 0
    is true).
     *  To show it rigorously for all m>=1, let us take m=1 and check the
    inequality again, P_1 = 26/30 > 1/8 and it is true. Let's think about the
    behaviour when m approaches infinity by dividing numerator and denominator
    of P_m by m^2. This would leave us with (12 + 12/m + 2/m^2) /
    (16+12/m+2/m^2) as m approaches infinity which is 12/16 = 3/4 > 1/8. Since
    this is also true, we can say that as m increases P_m will converge to 3/4
    and will never dip below 1/8 and therefore P_m>1/8.
    **In Summary**
    1.  We established that only pairs (i,j) with different remainders modulo 4
    lead to (i,j)-divisible sequences.
    2.  We counted the number of such pairs by breaking down the possible
    remainders when dividing the sequence index by 4.
    3.  We computed the probability P_m as the ratio of (favorable combinations
    of indices)/(total combination of indices).
    4. We showed that P_m > 1/8 by simplifying the equation and observing that
    all terms are positive and the condition hold for m=1, and that it
    approaches 3/4 as m goes to infinity.
